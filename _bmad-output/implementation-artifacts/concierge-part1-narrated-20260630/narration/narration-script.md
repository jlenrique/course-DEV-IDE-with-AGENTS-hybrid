## Card 3 — The Lineage of Physician Innovators  *(unchanged — FOLLOWS)*

Physicians have always been architects of change — and innovation in healthcare spans care delivery, informatics, system redesign, process science, and digital health. Consider five examples. During the pandemic, emergency physicians rapidly implemented tele-triage to evaluate patients remotely, reduce exposure risk, and preserve hospital capacity — innovation in real time. Clinicians are also innovating through informatics: the expansion of electronic medical records, and tools like Abridge, which uses AI to summarize clinical conversations — improving documentation efficiency, reducing burnout, and enhancing patient communication. Under Glenn Steele, Geisinger Health System became a model for system-level innovation: ProvenCare bundled evidence-based protocols with outcome guarantees, while ProvenHealth Navigator embedded nurse care managers into primary care. Atul Gawande recognized that even highly skilled surgeons make preventable errors in complex systems; by adapting an aviation-style checklist for surgery, he led a process innovation that reduced complications and mortality worldwide. And Michelle Longmire, a dermatologist, saw how traditional clinical trials excluded patients due to geographic and logistical barriers — so she founded Medable to support decentralized trials, bringing research into patients' homes and making studies faster and more inclusive.

## Card 4 — Expectations & Mental Frameworks  *(unchanged — FOLLOWS)*

To navigate this lineage, you must cultivate two foundational elements: a Growth Mindset, and Psychological Safety. A growth mindset, coined by Carol Dweck, is the belief that your capacity to innovate is not fixed — it's a muscle, built through effort and critique. But a growth mindset can't survive without psychological safety. Amy Edmondson defines this as a shared belief that the environment is safe for interpersonal risk-taking. As you progress through this certificate, you'll be asked to share half-baked ideas, critique your peers, and expose systemic flaws. We must collectively commit to maintaining psychological safety in our discussion boards and peer reviews. If we can't be vulnerable about what's broken, we can't design what's next.

## Card 6 — The Tightrope & The Net (Psychological Safety)  *(unchanged — FOLLOWS)*

We defined psychological safety as a prerequisite for innovation — but let's visualize why. Imagine asking your clinical team to walk a tightrope: to speak up against a senior attending, or propose a radical new workflow. If there's no safety net, they'll stick to the script. ## Card 7 — The "Wicked" World of Innovation (David Epstein)  *(unchanged — FOLLOWS)*

As clinical professionals, you've been rewarded for hyper-specialization. You've mastered 'Kind' learning environments, where patterns repeat and the rules are clear. But the systemic problems in healthcare are 'Wicked' environments — and to solve them, you must embrace functional flexibility. Watch this breakdown by David Epstein, 'Why the Most Experienced People Fail First,' on why generalists — those who can borrow tools from multiple domains — triumph in complex systems. The takeaway is this: to be an effective healthcare intrapreneur, you can't just be a hammer looking for a nail. You must have 'Range.' Functional flexibility means having the humility to drop the specific tools that made you a great clinician, and adopt the broad, interdisciplinary tools needed to redesign a broken system.
