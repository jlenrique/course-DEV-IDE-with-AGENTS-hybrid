## slide-2-the-human-cost-system-waste-burnout

# **Slide 2: The Human Cost: System Waste & Burnout**

- **Visual Format:** Infographic with two stark, contrasting statistics. [evidence: src-007]
- **Narration (Speaker Notes):** "These massive, complex systems are currently incredibly inefficient. Roughly 25% of total U.S. healthcare spending goes toward administrative costs and waste, not patient care. But the true cost of this inefficiency is human. Over half of physicians report symptoms of burnout. Crucially, the top driver of this burnout is not patient volume; it is bureaucratic tasks and administrative bloat. This is a profound insight. The burnout epidemic is not a personal failing of resilience; it is a system design problem. The workarounds we invent daily to survive a poorly designed EHR or a broken admission process are the exact friction points that are ripe for innovation." [evidence: src-007]

**References:** Shrank WH et al., JAMA (2019); Medscape Physician Burnout Report (2024). [evidence: src-007]

## slide-3-the-knowledge-explosion-new-technologies

# **Slide 3: The Knowledge Explosion & New Technologies**

- **Visual Format:** Conceptual Animation or Timeline Diagram. [evidence: src-008]
- **Narration (Speaker Notes):** "At the same time, the tools we use are evolving faster than our ability to adapt. In 1950, it took 50 years for medical knowledge to double; today, that doubling time is projected at just 73 days. We can no longer rely on static training. Furthermore, 66% of physicians are now using some form of AI in practice, yet formal training on how to oversee these tools safely remains severely limited. But the upside is tremendous. We see digital health innovations, like the SMART remote monitoring program, significantly reducing ER visits for COPD and heart failure by shifting care from reactive to proactive. We also see virtual reality transforming procedural training by allowing learners to rehearse complex scenarios without patient risk. Technology will reshape care, but it requires clinical innovators to ensure it does so safely and effectively." [evidence: src-008]

**References:** Densen P. (2011); Isaranuwatchai W et al., JMIR (2018); AMA AI Report. [evidence: src-008]

## slide-5-the-leadership-gap

# **Slide 5: The Leadership Gap**

- **Visual Format:** Bar chart highlighting the gap between "Interest in Leadership" and "Formal Training." [evidence: src-010]
- **Narration (Speaker Notes):** "This brings us to the ultimate gap, and the reason you are here. The data shows that 67% of practicing physicians want to pursue leadership roles to have a voice in organizational decisions. Yet, only 18% receive any executive or business training, and very few organizations offer formal leadership development. The COVID-19 pandemic clearly demonstrated that management and systems-thinking skills, often viewed as 'soft,' are actually critical to health and well-being. Academic Health Centers face this acutely—they have the clinical expertise but often lack the cultural incentives and interdisciplinary integration to translate ideas into scalable improvements. We need individuals who can bridge clinical practice with business and technology strategy." [evidence: src-010]
