> Let's turn to the physician innovator lineage — a heritage that already belongs to you. Before this course asks you to innovate, it asks you to recognize where you already stand. Physician innovation is not new — it has a lineage, and four distinct archetypes. Device innovators like Fogarty channeled surgical empathy and domain expertise — turned a clinical observation of a patient in crisis into the balloon catheter. Process innovators like Pronovost engineered a five-step checklist that cut ICU infection rates to near zero, where others saw an irreducible limitation — the raw material was already in their hands. Technology innovators like Bath filed a patent born from clinical domain expertise that changed ophthalmology. And organizational innovators redesigned entire systems from reactive sick care toward proactive health assurance. Your archetype is already inside this lineage. Moving forward, we'll examine what each archetype built.

#### apc-c1m1-tejal-20260419b-motion-card-10
<!-- REVOICE-REQUIRED: writer (Paige/Sophia) -> self-contained read-prose; Irene validates -->
> In this section, two frameworks anchor everything that follows. Growth Mindset — Dweck's formulation: the belief that your capacity to innovate is a muscle built through effort and critique, not a fixed trait. And Psychological Safety — Edmondson's definition: a shared belief that the environment is safe for interpersonal risk-taking — sharing half-baked ideas, critiquing peers, exposing systemic flaws. A growth mindset cannot survive without psychological safety. As you progress through this clinical course, these frameworks will be applied — along with a third framework, First Principles Thinking — first: break every assumption down to its fundamental truths, then reason upward to novel solutions. That chain from concept through process to outcome is the engine this course gives you for every clinical problem you bring. Moving forward, these conditions make your innovation work possible.
