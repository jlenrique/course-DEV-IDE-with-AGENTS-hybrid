# Innovation Leader Playbook Entry #1

**Format:** The first entry in the learner's personal Innovation Leader Playbook, a living document the learner can return to throughout the program.

**Instruction:** Include reasoning and thought process for every step.

## Task 1 - Evaluate: My Innovator's Starting Point

**Prompt:** Critically evaluate your own mindset, motivations, and work environment to establish a baseline for your innovation journey.

- **Goals and Fears:** What is your primary goal for taking this certificate program? What is one fear or apprehension you have about this process?
- **Optimizing Your Experience:** How can you proactively structure your time or mindset to get the most out of this program?
- **Personal SWOT Analysis:** Complete a personal SWOT analysis related to your capacity for innovation. In the Weaknesses section, identify one aspect of a fixed mindset you sometimes exhibit. In the Opportunities section, describe the current level of psychological safety on your primary team and identify one opportunity to improve it.

**Source note:** The source references a downloadable Personal Innovation SWOT Worksheet `.docx`, but the actual file is not present in this curated Part 4 corpus.

**Instructional Reasoning:** This task operationalizes Dweck's Growth Mindset and Edmondson's Psychological Safety. Naming fears and fixed-mindset traits disarms the traditional medical ego and establishes a ground for vulnerability and growth. Tying threats and opportunities to team psychological safety connects personal introspection to systemic awareness.

## Task 2 - Create: My Initial Idea Portfolio

**Prompt:** Source the initial seeds of innovation from your own experience. Briefly describe three distinct ideas, one from each category:

- **The Future Idea:** Describe a problem you would like to solve or an idea you would like to work on in the future.
- **The Past Idea:** Describe a problem or project you have worked on in the past. What went well? What would you do differently now? How did a lack of stakeholder alignment or an unclear value proposition contribute to the outcome?
- **The Observed Idea:** Describe an interesting project or innovation you have seen a colleague or another department work on. What did you find compelling about it?

**Instructional Reasoning:** This uses scaffolded recall. It shows physicians they are not starting from zero. Categorizing ideas into Future, Past, and Observed prevents blank-page paralysis.

## Task 3 - Create: My Opportunity Radar

**Design Brief 5: The Opportunity Radar**

- **Core Message:** You do not need a stroke of genius to innovate; you need to systematically scan three specific areas of your daily environment.
- **Visual Layout:** A top-down view of a glowing radar screen divided into three pie-slice zones.
- **Composition:** A classic circular radar interface with a sweeping green line. A physician/leader icon sits at the center. The radar is sectioned into three zones representing Past, Present, and Future, with small glowing blips representing opportunities.
- **Color Palette:** Dark, high-tech background with vibrant clinical-green or cyan radar lines and text.
- **Required Text Labels:**
  - Zone 1: "Looking Back: Past Frustrations & Failures"
  - Zone 2: "Looking Around: Present Workarounds"
  - Zone 3: "Looking Ahead: Future Trends & Mandates"
- **Speaker Notes:** "You now know how to diagnose a root cause. But where do you find the problems worth solving? You do not need to wait for a stroke of genius; you just need to turn on your radar. To find your portfolio project, systematically scan three zones: Look back at your past frustrations, look around at the workarounds your nurses are currently using to survive the shift, and look ahead to future hospital mandates. The friction is already on the screen; you just need to choose which blip to focus on."

**Prompt:** Establish your Opportunity Radar, which will be the foundation for your work throughout this course. Observe your daily work environment and identify five problems, frictions, or opportunities for improvement. These should be specific to your life as a physician. For each item, write a one-sentence problem statement.

**Examples:**

- The process for consenting patients for routine procedures is manual, redundant, and consumes too much resident time.
- There is no standardized way for attending physicians to give residents meaningful feedback on their clinical documentation in the EHR.
- Patients frequently miss their post-op appointments because the scheduling and reminder system is confusing.
- Our department's grand rounds are poorly attended because the content is not perceived as relevant to daily practice.

**Instructional Reasoning:** This translates the macro-level trillion-dollar healthcare problem into the learner's micro-level daily reality. Generating five distinct statements forces useful volume in the Ideate phase.
